What's so bad about plurality voting?
Vote splitting. Which in turn causes partisanship. If three or more candidates are running, and if two of them appeal to mostly the same voters, they split the vote, creating a disadvantage for both of them. Ralph Nader is well known for this issue, since he was seen as more similar to Gore than to Bush, and was generally seen to be a "spoiler." But this causes much, much bigger problems than the occasional election where there is a spoiler. Vote splitting results in parties dominating politics. The main purpose of parties is to give a strategic advantage by protecting their candidates against vote splitting. The Democrats didn't put both Hillary Clinton and Obama on the ballot (in 2008), instead they had primaries so only one of them was on the ballot. If they had allowed both to be on the ballot (along with all the other Democrat candidates), they would have lost to the Republicans, which were presumably smart enough to only have one candidate. The result is that things are artificially forced into two dominant opposing parties. (this is known as "Duverger's Law") Candidates who are more toward the center -- ones who might be the first choice of the hypothetical median voter -- end up in unelectable "no man's land." This results in a government that is often paralyzed by partisanship. All the ugly fighting you see between members of the two main opposing parties is directly caused by this. Dirty politics, scandals, etc. Politicians succeed not so much by being better at doing their job, but by tearing down the other politicians. You think that's just human nature? Not really....it's just the math of a bad voting system playing out. Things would be very different if we had a better voting system that wasn't so prone to vote splitting....several candidates could run without anyone being a "spoiler," and those that get elected would tend to be more toward the center (or, technically, it would tend to elect the candidate that was the first choice of the hypothetical "median voter"). Negative politics would rarely be a wise strategic move, and our elected officials would have far more incentive to cooperate with one another rather than only cooperating with those "on the same team." The only downside: elections would be a bit more boring....but that's ok. I believe this would allow things to run much more smoothly and effectively, as well as better reflecting the ideals of democracy.

Voting systems that use ranked ballots Various types of ranked voting ballot Ranked voting is any voting system that uses voters' rankings of candidates to choose a single winner or multiple winners. More formally, a ranked vote system depends only on voters' order of preference of the candidates. Ranked voting systems vary dramatically in how preferences are tabulated and counted, which gives them very different properties . In instant-runoff voting (IRV) and the single transferable vote system (STV), lower preferences are used as contingencies (back-up preferences) and are only applied when all higher-ranked preferences on a ballot have been eliminated or when the vote has been cast for a candidate who has been elected and surplus votes need to be transferred. Ranked votes of this type do not suffer the problem that a marked lower preference may be used against a voter's higher marked preference. Some ranked vote systems use ranks as weights; these systems are called positional voting . In the Borda method , the 1st, 2nd, 3rd... candidates on each ballot receive 1, 2, 3... points, and the candidate with the fewest points is elected. Thus intensity of preference is assumed to be at ratios of 1 to 2, 2 to 3, etc. In the United States and Australia, the terms ranked-choice voting and preferential voting , respectively, almost always [ according to whom? ] refer to instant-runoff voting ; however, because these terms have also been used to mean ranked systems in general, many social choice theorists recommend the use of the term instant-runoff voting in contexts where confusion might arise. Ranked votes do not incorporate any information about intensity of preferences . Furthermore, common implementations do not account for equality of preference among two or more candidates. Ranked voting systems of the instant-runoff voting type and the Borda count type are contrasted with rated voting methods, which allow voters to indicate how strongly they support different candidates (e.g. on a scale from 0 to 10). [ 1 ] Ranked vote systems produce more information than X voting systems such as first-past-the-post voting . Rated voting systems produce more information than ordinal ballots; as a result, some common results like Arrow's theorem do not directly apply to them. [ 2 ] Some ranked voting systems require the voter rank a set number of candidates. Others, such as optional preferential voting , allow the voter full liberty as to how many candidates they rank. Under STV or IRV, not all rankings are used in any case. Our polarized, partisan government, which is a direct result of a broken voting system.

Plurality voting (where more than two candidates can run, voters can vote for only one, and the one with the most votes wins) is very susceptible to vote splitting: i.e. similar candidates harm one another by both being on the ballot. Parties exist mostly to protect their candidates from vote splitting. Eventually things stabilize on two dominant parties, with those in the reasonable middle ground marginalized.

From then on, it's not about solving problems, but bringing down the other side.

The resulting infighting completely cripples our government in so many ways. Want to know why our health care system is crap? Why we have so much trouble fixing the economy? Why we can't get things like campaign finance reform to be implemented in a way that works? Why the US had a Civil War (causing hundreds of thousands of deaths) rather than being able to resolve differences peacefully? Ultimately it all can be traced back to our broken voting system.

There are much better systems, of course. Approval voting, ranked voting, etc. Unfortunately, people are so caught up in our current system, so busy being angry at the evil Republicans or the stupid Democrats, that they can't see that the real problem -- partisanship -- is so easy to fix with a straightforward change to the rules.

Do you think that the two-party system and the Electoral College should be reformed, and possibly even abolished, at least in the future? Why or why not?

The electoral college has some advantages, and some disadvantages. I don’t personally like it, but it is hard to get rid of, so it’s sort of an “it is what it is” situation. Also, keep in mind it applies to one single election in the US, not to all the elections for congress, senate, governors, etc.

The two party system, though, isn’t written into law, so you can’t really “abolish” it directly. But it is absolutely problematic, to a degree that I believe is currently crisis level. [*]

The main reason it exists as it does (where there are many parties, but in most elections the two front runners are in either the republican party or the democratic party) is because there is an advantage, due to our voting system, in clustering into factions and nominating a single candidate to represent your faction. Think of what would have happened if, in the last presidential election, all of these candidates were on the ballot? Obviously, none of them would have won, because the votes for the left would have been split between 20-some candidates.

Instead, they agreed to have a primary and pick just one (which was Biden) to put on the ballot. This was smart strategy, of course, and while many voters on the left didn’t get their first choice, they got a better result than if their first choice was actually on the ballot in the general election.

In the long term, this process tends to converge on two, equally powerful parties, because, well, math. ( Duverger's law - Wikipedia ) You can’t outlaw mathematical consequences.

So, yes, I think the 2-party system is bad and should be a thing of the past. But you don’t get there by abolishing it per se, you get there by having a voting system that doesn’t split the vote and tends toward the “first choice of the median voter”. Election result affecting losing candidate In social choice theory and politics , a spoiler effect happens when a losing candidate affects the results of an election simply by participating. [ 1 ] Voting rules that are not affected by spoilers are said to be spoilerproof , [ 2 ] [ 3 ] and satisfy independence of irrelevant alternatives . [ 1 ] [ 3 ] [ 4 ] The frequency and severity of spoiler effects depends substantially on the voting method. First-past-the-post voting without winnowing or primary elections [ citation needed ] is sensitive to spoilers. And so, to a degree, are instant-runoff or ranked-choice voting (RCV) and the two-round system (TRS). [ 4 ] [ 5 ] [ 6 ] [ 7 ] Majority-rule (or Condorcet) methods are only rarely affected by spoilers, which are limited to rare situations, [ 8 ] [ 9 ] called cyclic ties . [ 8 ] [ 9 ] [ 10 ] Rated voting systems are not subject to Arrow's theorem , allowing them to be spoilerproof so long as voters' ratings are consistent across elections. [ 2 ] [ 11 ] [ 12 ] [ 13 ] Spoiler effects can also occur in some methods of proportional representation , such as the single transferable vote (STV or RCV-PR) and the largest remainders method of party-list representation, where it is called the new states paradox . A new party entering an election causes some seats to shift from one unrelated party to another, even if the new party wins no seats. [ 14 ] This kind of spoiler effect is avoided by divisor methods and proportional approval . [ 14 ] : Thm.8.3 In decision theory , independence of irrelevant alternatives is a fundamental principle of rational choice which says that a decision between two outcomes, A or B , should not depend on the quality of a third, unrelated outcome C . A famous joke by Sidney Morgenbesser illustrates this principle: [ 15 ] [ failed verification ] A man is deciding whether to order apple, blueberry, or cherry pie before settling on apple. The waitress informs him that the cherry pie is very good and a favorite of most customers. The man replies "in that case, I'll have the blueberry." Politicians and social choice theorists have long argued for the unfairness of spoiler effects. The mathematician and political economist Nicolas de Condorcet was the first to study the spoiler effect, in the 1780s. Approval voting - Wikipedia
Single-winner electoral system On an approval ballot , the voter can select any number of candidates. Approval voting is a single-winner rated voting system where voters approve of any number of candidates named on a ballot, and the candidate with the most votes is victorious. The method is designed to eliminate vote-splitting while keeping election administration simple and easy to count , requiring only a single score for each candidate. Proponents of approval voting argue that the system more accurately represents candidates not belonging to a major party . [ 1 ] While approval voting is primarily considered in single-winner contexts, application to multiple-winner contexts is also possible. For example, if there are ten seats to be filled, the ten most-voted candidates would be selected. [ 2 ] Winners can be selected in a single round of approval voting, but preliminary " narrowing " rounds can be included. Approval voting has been used in both organizational and political elections to improve representativeness and voter satisfaction. As the number of candidates a voter may approve of is unlimited, approval voting is immune to overvoting . Critics of approval voting have argued the simple ballot format is a disadvantage, as it forces a binary choice for each candidate in place of the expressive grades of other rated voting rules. However, supporters of approval voting see grading as necessitating strategic voting and complicating the job of the voter. Effect on elections [ edit ] Research by social choice theorists Steven Brams and Dudley R. Herschbach found that approval voting would increase voter participation, prevent minor-party candidates from being spoilers , and reduce negative campaigning. [ 3 ] Brams' research concluded that approval can be expected to elect majority-preferred candidates in practical election scenarios, avoiding the center squeeze common to ranked-choice voting and primary elections . [ 4 ] [ 5 ] One study showed that approval would not have chosen the same two winners as plurality voting ( Jacques Chirac and Jean-Marie Le Pen ) in the first round of the 2002 French presidential election ; it instead would have chosen Chirac and Lionel Jospin as the top two candidates to proceed to the runoff. In the actual election, Le Pen lost by an overwhelming margin in the runoff, 82.2% to 17.8%, a sign that the true top two candidates had not been found. In the approval voting survey primary, Chirac took first place with 36.7%, compared to Jospin at 32.9%. Le Pen, in that study, received 25.1% and so would not have made the cut to the second round. In the real primary election, the top three were Chirac, 19.9%, Le Pen, 16.9%, and Jospin, 16.2%. Single-winner electoral system The STAR voting ballot, including recommended instructions and formatting details Graphic explaining how STAR Voting works STAR voting is an electoral system for single-seat elections. [ 1 ] [ 2 ] The name (an allusion to star ratings ) stands for " Score Then Automatic Runoff ", referring to the fact that this system is a combination of score voting , to pick two finalists with the highest total scores, followed by an "automatic runoff" in which the finalist who is preferred on more ballots wins. It is a type of cardinal voting electoral system. In STAR, voters are given a score ballot (or ratings ballot ) on which each voter scores candidates with a number from 0 up to 5, with 0 representing "worst" and 5 representing "best". The scores for each candidate are then summed, and the two highest-scored candidates are selected as finalists. In the automatic runoff round, the finalist who was given a higher score on a greater number of ballots is selected as the winner. The concept was first proposed in October 2014 by Mark Frohnmayer , and was initially called score runoff voting ( SRV ). [ 3 ] The runoff step was introduced in an attempt to reduce strategic incentives in ordinary score voting, such as bullet voting and tactical maximization. [ 4 ] STAR is intended to be a hybrid between (rated) score voting and (ranked) instant runoff voting . There are a lot of reasons people cite for wanting ranked choice voting, but reducing polarization is by far the one that matters most.

The US is as polarized as it has been since the Civil War. There is no reasonable argument that this is a good thing. It’s not a healthy hashing out of issues, it’s just ugliness.

Ranked voting methods ... whether they be regular “Instant Runoff” or the theoretically better “Condorcet compliant” variations … as well as other options such as Approval voting, STAR voting, and Score voting, address polarization. This answer of mine from 5 years ago explains it in a few short paragraphs. If that is too long for you, the TLDR; is: regular voting results in vote splitting, and parties must nominate a single candidate to avoid losing because of it. That pushes out candidates that appeal to the middle ground voters.

How does ranked choice voting help to lessen polarization between political parties?
Plurality voting (that is, “normal” voting) is subject to vote splitting, where things get wonky with more than two candidates. So they have primaries or party nominations to protect similar candidates from splitting the vote. In the end, there will tend to be two major parties, and two candidates, one representing the “right” and one the “left.” Candidates in the middle — for instance, a candidate that is the first choice of the median voter — are essentially unelectable. They won’t get a party’s support, because the whole point of a party is to “pull things in their direction,” so they aren’t going to pick a candidate that is right in the middle. With ranked ballots, it is a lot more likely the candidate that appeals to the median voter will be elected. There isn’t vote splitting, so candidates all across the board can run without damaging their own cause. Political parties become far less necessary (and independent candidates would be much more common) Those toward the middle actually have the best chance of winning, everything else being equal.

Are people too politically tribalistic to benefit from an electoral system based on popular vote?
Rob Brown
Software developer with long obsession with voting systems · 6 y ·

Depends on the voting system used. If it is using plurality (otherwise known as “First Past The Post”), where voters pick one candidate and the one with the most votes wins, yes, we are too tribal. In fact, that system exaggerates the tribalistic tendencies of the electorate. [1]

If we were to vote using a better system, which tended to elect the first choice of the median voter [2], we’d be fine. But under plurality, things get highly polarized and tend to break down, and we are seeing that now in the US.

1. due to vote splitting, and the incentive to cluster into parties to protect against losing due to vote splitting, and the subsequent tendency (Duverger’s Law) to settle onto two dominant parties.

2. “median voter” isn’t always easy to define, so it is a hypothetical. But it is easy to visualize in the contrived case of voting for a numerical value, such as a budget or the temperature to set the thermostat to. Everyone votes for their “most preferred value”, and the median choice is selected. No incentive to exaggerate, no incentive to cluster into parties, etc. This ideal should be aimed for even when electing human candidates. Approval voting, Star Voting, and Condorcet voting methods all do a pretty good job at electing the first choice of the median voter. Ranked Choice voting is better than plurality, but fairly flawed.

Why do we not use ranked voting in the United States?
Rob Brown
Software developer with long obsession with voting systems · 6 y ·

Because most people don’t understand the problems with our current system, which drives people into two opposing parties and promotes tribalism. (which we are currently seeing do immense damage to our country)

That said, ranked voting, especially if it is tabulated as “instant runoff,” has problems of its own, not the least of which the additional cognitive burden it places on voters. Probably a better solution is approval voting (where you vote for as many candidates as you wish, rather than having to pick just one). Neither are perfect, but almost anything is dramatically better than the current system used for almost all elections in the US.

The real enemy is vote splitting, which tends to squeeze out moderate/centrist candidates. The reason is that, to prevent vote splitting causing harm to their side, people cluster into political parties, and there is a very predictable mathematical tendency toward these parties narrowing down to two dominant parties. Middle ground is a no-mans land.
