Originally Answered: Is there a rational middle ground between Democratic and Republican views of government? · ·

This polarization is a direct consequence of the way we vote, specifically Plurality voting, where each voter casts a vote for a single candidate out of a field of many, and the candidate with the most votes wins. The problem with this system is that two candidates who are seen as similar -- that is they tend to appeal to the same set of voters -- split the vote, creating a severe disadvantage if both are on the ballot. That is why parties form, and why they nominate a single candidate rather than allowing several of them to be on the ballot.

Over time, this tends to settle on two dominant, opposing parties. Anything other than two is not stable. This means that voters whose opinions most closely align with the position of one or the other dominant party have the most power, while those in the center don't really get to have much of a say in elections. As time goes on, the voters themselves tend to become similarly polarized. Being in the middle becomes harder and harder.


So yes, there is a middle ground on almost everything. If we actually elected such middle ground candidates -- those with more nuanced stances that approximated that of the median voter -- we'd have a lot more effective government, in my opinion. We don't have that -- not because of human nature, not because of an intrinsic lack of a middle ground -- but because our voting system is badly flawed. (and yes this can be fixed... see )

Originally Answered: What are you amazed that more people are not upset about? · ·

Our polarized, partisan government, which is a direct result of a broken voting system.

Plurality voting (where more than two candidates can run, voters can vote for only one, and the one with the most votes wins) is very susceptible to vote splitting: i.e. similar candidates harm one another by both being on the ballot. Parties exist mostly to protect their candidates from vote splitting. Eventually things stabilize on two dominant parties, with those in the reasonable middle ground marginalized.

From then on, it's not about solving problems, but bringing down the other side.

The resulting infighting completely cripples our government in so many ways. Want to know why our health care system is crap? Why we have so much trouble fixing the economy? Why we can't get things like campaign finance reform to be implemented in a way that works? Why the US had a Civil War (causing hundreds of thousands of deaths) rather than being able to resolve differences peacefully? Ultimately it all can be traced back to our broken voting system.

Do you think that the two-party system and the Electoral College should be reformed, and possibly even abolished, at least in the future? Why or why not?

The electoral college has some advantages, and some disadvantages. I don’t personally like it, but it is hard to get rid of, so it’s sort of an “it is what it is” situation. Also, keep in mind it applies to one single election in the US, not to all the elections for congress, senate, governors, etc.

The two party system, though, isn’t written into law, so you can’t really “abolish” it directly. But it is absolutely problematic, to a degree that I believe is currently crisis level. [*]

The main reason it exists as it does (where there are many parties, but in most elections the two front runners are in either the republican party or the democratic party) is because there is an advantage, due to our voting system, in clustering into factions and nominating a single candidate to represent your faction. Think of what would have happened if, in the last presidential election, all of these candidates were on the ballot? Obviously, none of them would have won, because the votes for the left would have been split between 20-some candidates.

Instead, they agreed to have a primary and pick just one (which was Biden) to put on the ballot. This was smart strategy, of course, and while many voters on the left didn’t get their first choice, they got a better result than if their first choice was actually on the ballot in the general election.

Same goes for the right. They wouldn’t have gotten their choice in 2016 if all these candidates were on the general election ballot:

In the long term, this process tends to converge on two, equally powerful parties, because, well, math. ( Duverger's law - Wikipedia ) You can’t outlaw mathematical consequences.

So, yes, I think the 2-party system is bad and should be a thing of the past. But you don’t get there by abolishing it per se, you get there by having a voting system that doesn’t split the vote and tends toward the “first choice of the median voter”. STAR and Approval voting are both good alternatives, while Ranked Choice (another name for “Instant Runoff voting”) sort of claims to do this but unfortunately doesn’t do it as well as you might think.

Are people too politically tribalistic to benefit from an electoral system based on popular vote?
Software developer with long obsession with voting systems 6 y
Rob Brown
· ·

Depends on the voting system used. If it is using plurality (otherwise known as “First Past The Post”), where voters pick one candidate and the one with the most votes wins, yes, we are too tribal. In fact, that system exaggerates the tribalistic tendencies of the electorate. [1]

If we were to vote using a better system, which tended to elect the first choice of the median voter [2], we’d be fine. But under plurality, things get highly polarized and tend to break down, and we are seeing that now in the US.

1. due to vote splitting, and the incentive to cluster into parties to protect against losing due to vote splitting, and the subsequent tendency (Duverger’s Law) to settle onto two dominant parties.

2. “median voter” isn’t always easy to define, so it is a hypothetical. But it is easy to visualize in the contrived case of voting for a numerical value, such as a budget or the temperature to set the thermostat to. Everyone votes for their “most preferred value”, and the median choice is selected. No incentive to exaggerate, no incentive to cluster into parties, etc. This ideal should be aimed for even when electing human candidates. Approval voting, Star Voting, and Condorcet voting methods all do a pretty good job at electing the first choice of the median voter. Ranked Choice voting is better than plurality, but fairly flawed.

Why do we not use ranked voting in the United States?
Software developer with long obsession with voting systems 6 y
Rob Brown
· ·

Because most people don’t understand the problems with our current system, which drives people into two opposing parties and promotes tribalism. (which we are currently seeing do immense damage to our country)

That said, ranked voting, especially if it is tabulated as “instant runoff,” has problems of its own, not the least of which the additional cognitive burden it places on voters. Probably a better solution is approval voting (where you vote for as many candidates as you wish, rather than having to pick just one). Neither are perfect, but almost anything is dramatically better than the current system used for almost all elections in the US.

The real enemy is vote splitting, which tends to squeeze out moderate/centrist candidates. The reason is that, to prevent vote splitting causing harm to their side, people cluster into political parties, and there is a very predictable mathematical tendency toward these parties narrowing down to two dominant parties. Middle ground is a no-mans land.
