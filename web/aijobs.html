AI Is Going to Take Your Job.
Yes, Yours.

By J. Carter Published Oct 2024 6 Min Read

You saw the headline. You’re already building your defense.

Maybe you work in a specialized industry—medicine, corporate law, systems engineering, operations. You've likely filed arguments like this away under "tech hype," confident that automated algorithms are only bound to displace repetitive, manual labor.

Let's shift focus to graphics and cinema. Not because Hollywood represents the whole workforce, but because it is where the capability transition is most visible. It functions as a clear proof of concept for what is coming—and points directly to your field.

The Creative Jobs Were Supposed to Be Safe

For decades, the common consensus argued that automation would first conquer routine physical tasks—assembly lines, logistics, basic inventory—while creative decision-making and knowledge work remained safe behind human intelligence. We had that entirely backwards.

And those who felt most secure pointed to a single, recurring flaw as proof: hands.

Early image generators famously struggled with hands. Fingers were fused together, bent at unnatural angles, or grew in sets of six. It became a running industry joke—a reliable way to spot a synthetic image and reassure yourself that human designers were safe.

“But That’s Just Entertainment”

This is the most frequent pushback. The arts and entertainment fields are treated as separate from "substantive" industries.

But the professionals building these creative assets are not casual users. They are highly trained technical experts who spent decades mastering complex software and spatial math. Their roles are being condensed into prompt systems and real-time generation. If a neural model can replicate aesthetic balance, geometric consistency, and lighting logic, why assume your field is structured differently?

The underlying system is general, not application-specific. It did not learn to process video because a programmer wrote an explicit video-rendering algorithm; it developed the capability by processing structural data. That same general learning model is being adapted to software engineering, medical imagery analysis, contract drafting, and financial forecasting.

In software development—the field where my experience lies—automated engineering capabilities are actually progressing faster than visual generation tools. They simply do not look as flashy in a video presentation.

Robots, Not Just Software

If you find comfort in the belief that your work requires physical actions in the real world, you should notice that the hardware gap is narrowing as well.

Play: Autonomous Robotics Demo

The manufacturing cost of humanoid and specialized robotic hardware is dropping alongside the scale-up in AI reasoning. Combining spatial reasoning with physical mechanical units is changing the outlook for manufacturing, logistics, complex assembly, and care settings.

Businesses adopt these systems out of practical necessity rather than hostility. If your direct competitor lowers operational overhead by eighty percent, your options are to automate or exit the market. No enterprise can opt out of this shift, much like businesses couldn't opt out of adopting computers in the nineties.

So What Do We Do?

This is where public debate tends to drift toward fatalism, which misinterprets the challenge. This is not a production problem; it is a distribution problem.

Automation does not reduce actual abundance—it dramatically increases it. The core challenge is that we have historically relied on forty hours of weekly labor as the primary mechanism for distributing resources to individuals. If labor becomes abundant and inexpensive, we need to adapt our economic framework to preserve consumer capacity while keeping capital active.

We need stable fiscal solutions, such as progressive tax adjustments tied directly to computing output alongside mechanisms resistant to simple lobbying. This transition does not have to result in destitution; it requires updating outdated systems to match modern efficiency.
